Definition

Sometimes statistics lie, but more often humans mishear them. Like with Simpson's Paradox, which Wikipedia defines as

a paradox in which a trend that appears in different groups of data disappears when these groups are combined, and the reverse trend appears for the aggregate data.

Simpson's paradox is especially pernicious when we talk about causality. To the right, x appears to have a negative effect on y, but actually the opposite is true when you account for color.

Berkeley Sex Discrimination Suit

In 1973, University of California-Berkeley (where Lewis and Victor live) was sued for sex discrimination. Berkeley had just accepted 44% of male applicants but only 35% of female applicants.

When a statistician, an anthropologist and a staff data processor looked at the evidence, they found the opposite:

If the data are properly pooled...there is a small but statistically significant bias in favor of women.

(p. 403)

"Properly pooled" means "broken down by department." Men more often applied to science and engineering departments, which require special technical skills but accept a large percentage of qualified applicants of both sexes. Women more often applied to humanities departments, which have fewer spaces but require, as a minimum to apply, only a standard undergradaute cirriculum.

The authors concluded that any sexism occurred before Berkeley ever saw the applications:

Women are shunted by their socialization and education toward fields of graduate study that are generally more crowded, less productive of completed degrees, and less well funded, and that frequently offer poorer professional employment prospects
